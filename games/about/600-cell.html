600 Cell

The 600-cell is the generalization of the regular icosahedron in a space of four dimensions. It has billions of simple patterns in it that provide proofs of an important theorem in the foundations of quantum mechanics due to Bell, Kochen and Specker. This theorem rules out the existence of a class of deterministic theories that were proposed as alternatives to quantum mechanics. The patterns can be displayed as tables of numbers with a characteristic signature that makes them easy to recognize. The adjoining table allows all the patterns to be seen as subsets of the numbers in it.

It is necessary to be acquainted with a few facts about the 600-cell to recognize the patterns in it. The 600-cell has 120 vertices that lie on the surface of a four-dimensional sphere. The vertices come in antipodal pairs, each represented by a number from 1 to 60 in the adjoining table. The sets of four vertex pairs grouped together are in mutually orthogonal directions from the center of the sphere. There are 75 such sets, and each will be termed a basis.

The bases are grouped together in blocks of three, there being five blocks in every row and column of the table. Each vertex pair occurs once in every horizontal row of blocks, and also once in every vertical column. Thus, each vertex pair occurs five times throughout the table.

The patterns in the table that provide proofs of the Bell-Kochen-Specker theorem consist of all odd numbers of its bases with the property that each of the vertex pairs (or numbers) that occurs in them occurs in an even number of them. The patterns are thus just like the winning patterns in Peres-1 and Peres-2.

The 600-cell has well over a billion such patterns in it, and you can call up a few of them by using the pull-down menu. If you click on 30-15, for example, you will see 30 vertex pairs, each occurring twice, over the 15 bases of the pattern (30 x 2 = 15 x 4). Similarly, if you click on 48-25, you will see 46 vertex pairs, each occurring twice, and two vertex pairs, each occurring four times, over 25 bases (46 x 2 + 2 x 4 = 25 x 4). The patterns are highlighted in green to make them easy to pick out from the background.

You can check the validity of any pattern by noting that it consists of an odd number of bases and then hovering your cursor over any number in it to see that it occurs an even number of times over the pattern. The basis counter and scoreboard also allow you to check these features.

Instead of calling up a pattern, you could try making up one on your own. Just click on bases, using the basis counter and scoreboard to keep track of your progress as you go along. You can undo earlier moves at any point simply by clicking on lighted bases. You win only when the moves box and scoreboard are both lit up entirely in green, and the title at the top then changes to “God does play dice!” However, be warned that winning by trial and error is not easy!
