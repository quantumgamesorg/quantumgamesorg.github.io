The 120-cell

Note: This demonstration is not difficult to follow but requires more explanation than the earlier ones. Thus, it is aimed mainly at enthusiasts of the BKS theorem!

The 120-cell is the generalization of the regular dodecahedron in a space of four dimensions. Like the 600-cell and the two-qubit system, it has billions of simple patterns that give instant proofs of the Bell-Kochen-Specker theorem. However, the table of numbers in which the patterns are embedded is too large to be shown on a computer screen and so they will be presented here in a compressed form that nevertheless allows them to be recognized as genuine patterns.

You should be familiar with the exhibit for the 600-cell before looking at this one, as that will make it much easier to follow.

The 120-cell has 600 vertices lying on the surface of a sphere in four-dimensional space. The vertices come in antipodal pairs, and the line through any antipodal pair of vertices will be termed a ray. The rays will be numbered from 1 to 300 according to a scheme to be laid out shortly. A set of four rays in mutually orthogonal directions from the center of the 120-cell will be termed a basis, and a basis will be specified by the numbers of the four rays in it. The 300 rays of the 120-cell form 675 bases, with each ray occurring in exactly nine bases. The 120-cell can therefore be described by the ray-basis symbol 300_9 – 675_4, where the subscript on the left indicates the number of bases to which each ray belongs and that on the right the number rays in each basis; note that 300 x 9 = 675 x 4, so that the total ray count is the same whether done by rays or bases.

The numbering scheme we use for the rays is based on the “triacontagonal projection” of H.S.M.Coxeter, which is a way of casting a shadow of certain higher dimensional figures (of which the 120-cell is one) on a two-dimensional plane in such a way that their vertices lie on a number of concentric regular triacontagons (or 30-gons). Modifying the construction to apply to rays rather than vertices, we associate the rays, in sets of fifteen, with the vertices of twenty concentric regular pentadecagons (or 15-gons) that we label by the letters A through T. Rays 1-15 are associated with the vertices of A in counterclockwise order beginning from a chosen vertex, rays 16-30 with the vertices of B in a similar manner, and so on, and ending with rays 286-300 being associated with the vertices of T.

With this numbering of the rays, the bases become easy to specify because it turns out that adding one to each ray of a basis always yields another basis provided that one loops back to the ray at the beginning of a 15-gon when the one at the end is overshot. To understand better what this means, consider the basis consisting of the rays 19, 120, 224, 249. Adding one to each of these rays gives 20, 121, 225, 250, but this is not a basis because the ray 120 is the last member of the 15-gon H and must be replaced by the ray at the beginning of H, which is 106; thus the proper successor to 19, 120, 224, 249 is 20, 106, 225, 250. Adding one to each ray of 20, 106, 225, 250 gives 21, 107, 211, 251, and one can continue in this way to obtain a chain of fifteen bases that loops back on itself, in the sense that the last basis of the chain leads back to the one we started off with. It is clear that all the members of the chain can be generated from any one of them, and we will term the particular member we pick for this process the generator. The 120-cell has 45 generators, each of which gives rise to a chain of fifteen bases, and the 45 x 15 = 675 bases so obtained comprise all the bases of the 120-cell.

The generators of the 120-cell are shown below, with a lower case letter attached to each. Clicking on a generator will cause all the bases associated with it to appear in a pop up window. If you run your eye down any column of numbers in the window, you will see all the rays associated with the 15-gon whose label is indicated at the head of the column. Thus any generator can be specified by either its lower case letter or the set of four capital letters representing the 15-gons from which its rays are drawn. For example, generator a can also be written as ABST, generator b as ACRT, and so on. We will make use of this equivalence shortly.





The patterns in the 120-cell that prove the BKS theorem can be written as “words” made up of an odd number of distinct letters, each representing one of its 45 generators. The word must consist of an odd number of letters because each letter represents fifteen bases and the total number of bases associated with the word must be odd. However, not all odd-letter words represent patterns because of the additional requirement that each ray occur an even number of times over all the bases of the pattern. Since only complete 15-gons of rays occur in the words we are considering, the requirement that each ray occur an even number of times over the pattern is equivalent to the requirement that every capital letter occur an even number of times over all the lower case letters that make up a word.

This makes it easy to tell if a word represents a valid pattern or not. If it consists of an odd number of letters, it passes the first test. To apply the second test, we replace each lower case letter of the word by its set of four capital letters and then count the number of times each of the capital letters occurs over the word. If all the counts are even, the word passes the second test and represents a valid pattern.

As an example of this procedure, consider the word abegkri'. The word consists of seven letters and so passes the first test. The application of the second test is shown in the flowchart below:


   abegkri' → (ABST)(ACRT)(AFPT)(AHMT)(BEPS)(CFOR)(EHMO) → (BCEFHMOPRS)2(AT)4 → 1502304 – 1054

abegkri' → (ABST)(ACRT)(AFPT)(AHMT)(BEPS)(CFOR)(EHMO) → (BCEFHMOPRS)2(AT)4 → 1502304 – 1054
The first step can be accomplished with the help of the pop up windows for the generators, which show what the four capital letters of each are. In the second step, we collected together all letters occurring the same number of times over the word, attaching the number of occurrences as a subscript. At this stage we can tell if the word represents a pattern or not by seeing if all the subscripts are even; if they are it represents a pattern, otherwise not. In the final step we obtain the ray-basis formula of the pattern by replacing each string of letters within brackets by fifteen times the number of letters in the string and add a dash followed by the number of bases (which is fifteen times the number of letters in the word), with the subcript 4 attached (which indicates the number of rays in each basis). The formula could also be obtained by first recovering all the bases associated with the word and then doing a ray count over it, but the occurrence of entire 15-gons of rays in the word makes this more involved procedure unnecessary.

The task of picking out all odd-letter words that represent valid patterns is not straightforward. However it has been made easy for you. Simply enter a 0 or 1 in each of the 29 square boxes below (there are 15 boxes in the first row and 14 in the second) and click the set button to their right. This will make the letters of the pattern appear in the round boxes below the first set of square ones. Clicking the set button to the right of the round boxes will show how many times each of the capital letters occurs over the word in the set of 20 square boxes below the round ones. Even letter counts are shown on a green background and odd ones on a red background. This allows one to confirm at a glance that the pattern is a valid one simply by noting that all the boxes are green. Instead of clicking the set button after the round boxes, you can also click on the letters one by one and see the pattern build up. If you do so, you will see red boxes frequently during the buildup, but after all the letters have been clicked only green boxes will be seen.

Sometimes, a word representing a pattern may have a smaller word inside it that also represents a pattern. An example of this is the word cduvy which represents a pattern with the formula 1502 – 754. However, by dropping two of its letters one gets the shorter word cdy which represents a pattern with the formula 902 – 454. After you have called up a pattern, you can drop some of the letters in it (by clicking on them and turning them red) and then see if the green letters you are left with make up a pattern or not. It's a challenge to find long words representing patterns that don't have any smaller words representing patterns inside them. An example of such a word of nine letters is fghilmsup', which is a pattern with the formula 1802454 – 1354.

In closing, we mention that we have constructed only patterns of a special type, namely, those involving both rays and bases that are multiples of fifteen. The 120-cell houses a large number of other patterns that prove the BKS theorem, but they cannot be displayed using the approach adopted here.
