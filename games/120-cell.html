The 120-cell

Note: This demonstration is not difficult to follow but requires more explanation than the earlier ones. Thus, it is aimed mainly at enthusiasts of the BKS theorem!

The 120-cell is the generalization of the regular dodecahedron in a space of four dimensions. Like the 600-cell and the two-qubit system, it has billions of simple patterns that give instant proofs of the Bell-Kochen-Specker theorem. However, the table of numbers in which the patterns are embedded is too large to be shown on a computer screen and so they will be presented here in a compressed form that nevertheless allows them to be recognized as genuine patterns.

You should be familiar with the exhibit for the 600-cell before looking at this one, as that will make it much easier to follow.

The 120-cell has 600 vertices lying on the surface of a sphere in four-dimensional space. The vertices come in antipodal pairs, and the line through any antipodal pair will be termed a ray. The rays will be numbered from 1 to 300 according to a scheme to be laid out shortly. A set of four rays in mutually orthogonal directions from the center of the 120-cell will be termed a basis, and a basis will be specified by the numbers of the four rays in it. The 300 rays of the 120-cell form 675 bases, with each ray occurring in exactly nine bases. The 120-cell can therefore be described by the ray-basis symbol 300_9 – 675_4, where the subscript on the left indicates the number of bases to which each ray belongs and that on the right the number rays in each basis; note that 300 x 9 = 675 x 4, so that the total ray count is the same whether done by rays or bases.

The numbering scheme we use for the rays is based on the “triacontagonal projection” of H.S.M.Coxeter, which is a way of casting a shadow of certain higher dimensional figures (of which the 120-cell is one) on a two-dimensional plane in such a way that their vertices lie on a number of concentric regular triacontagons (or 30-gons). Modifying the construction to apply to rays rather than vertices, we associate the rays, in sets of fifteen, with the vertices of twenty concentric regular pentadecagons (or 15-gons) that we label by the letters A through T. Rays 1-15 are associated with the vertices of A in counterclockwise order beginning from a chosen ray, rays 16-30 with the vertices of B in a similar manner, and so on, and ending with rays 286-300 being associated with the vertices of T.

With this numbering of the rays, the bases become easy to specify because it turns out that adding one to each of the rays of a basis always yields another basis provided that one loops back to the ray at the beginning of a 15-gon when one overshoots the ray at the end. To understand what this means, consider the basis consisting of the rays 19, 120, 224, 249. Adding one to each of the rays gives 20, 121, 225, 250, but this is not the successor to the original basis because ray 120 is the last member of the 15-gon with the label H and must be followed by the ray at the beginning of H, which is 106; thus the successor to the original basis is 20, 106, 225, 250. From this example it should be clear that the bases break up into cycles of fifteen, with all the members of any cycle being obtainable from any one of them when its rays are subjected to repeated shifts of a single unit. The 120-cell has 45 completely disjoint cycles of fifteen generators each, and the 45 x 15 = 675 bases they give rise to comprise all the bases of the 120-cell.

The patterns in the 120-cell that prove the BKS theorem can be written as “words” made up of an odd number of (distinct) letters, each representing one of the 45 generators above. Since each letter represents a cycle of 15 bases, a word with an odd number of letters corresponds to an odd number of bases, which is one of the characteristics of a valid pattern. However, not all odd-letter words are valid patterns because they must also satisfy the requirement that each ray occur an even number of times over all the bases of the pattern. Since only complete 15-gons of rays occur in the bases of a pattern, the latter requirement can be rephrased as the requirement that the capital letters that occur in a word each occur an even number of times over all the letters of the word.
