The 120-cell

Note: This demonstration is not difficult to follow but requires more explanation than the earlier ones. Thus, it is aimed mainly at enthusiasts of the BKS theorem!

The 120-cell is the generalization of the regular dodecahedron in a space of four dimensions. Like the 600-cell and the two-qubit system, it has billions of simple patterns that give instant proofs of the Bell-Kochen-Specker theorem. However, the table of numbers in which the patterns are embedded is too large to be shown on a computer screen and so they will be presented here in a compressed form that nevertheless allows them to be recognized as genuine patterns.

You should be familiar with the exhibit for the 600-cell before looking at this one, as that will make it much easier to follow.

The 120-cell has 600 vertices lying on the surface of a sphere in four-dimensional space. The vertices come in antipodal pairs, and the line through any antipodal pair will be termed a ray. The rays will be numbered from 1 to 300 according to a scheme to be laid out shortly. A set of four rays in mutually orthogonal directions from the center of the 120-cell will be termed a basis, and a basis will be specified by the numbers of the four rays in it. The 300 rays of the 120-cell form 675 bases, with each ray occurring in exactly nine bases. The 120-cell can therefore be described by the ray-basis symbol 300_9 – 675_4, where the subscript on the left indicates the number of bases to which each ray belongs and that on the right the number rays in each basis; note that 300 x 9 = 675 x 4, so that the total ray count is the same whether done by rays or bases.

The numbering scheme we use for the rays is based on the “triacontagonal projection” of H.S.M.Coxeter, which is a way of casting a shadow of certain higher dimensional figures (of which the 120-cell is one) on a two-dimensional plane in such a way that their vertices lie on a number of concentric regular triacontagons (or 30-gons). Modifying the construction to apply to rays rather than vertices, we associate the rays, in sets of fifteen, with the vertices of twenty concentric regular pentadecagons (or 15-gons) that we label by the letters A through T. Rays 1-15 are associated with the vertices of A in counterclockwise order beginning from a chosen vertex, rays 16-30 with the vertices of B in a similar manner, and so on, with rays 286-300 being associated with the vertices of T.

With this numbering of the rays, the bases become easy to specify because it turns out that adding one to each ray of a basis always yields another basis provided that one loops back to the ray at the beginning of a 15-gon when one at the end is overshot. To understand what the rider in bold means, consider the basis consisting of the rays 19, 120, 224, 249. Adding one to each of these rays gives 20, 121, 225, 250, but this is not a basis because the ray 120 is the last member of the 15-gon with label H and must be replaced by the ray at the beginning of H, which is 106; thus the successor to the basis 19, 120, 224, 249 is 20, 106, 225, 250. It should be clear from this example that the bases come in cycles of fifteen, with all members of a cycle following from any one of them when its rays are shifted progressively by the same amonunt, subject to rider mentioned above. We will term the representative from each cycle from which all its other members can be obtained the generator. The 120-cell has 45 generators, each giving rise to a cycle of fifteen bases, and the 45 x 15 = 675 bases so obtained comprise all the bases of the 120-cell.

The generators of the 120-cell are shown below, with each being labeled by a lower case letter. Clicking on any generator will cause a pop-up window to appear to the right where all the bases resulting from it are listed. If you run your eye down any column of numbers in that list, you will see all the rays associated with the 15-gon whose label is indicated at the head of the column. Any cycle of fifteen bases can therefore be specified either by the lower case letter representing a generator or by the set of four capital letters representing the 15-gons out of which the rays of its bases are drawn; thus a = ABST, b = ACRT, and so on. This dual representation will be made use of shortly.





The patterns in the 120-cell that prove the BKS theorem can be written as “words” made up of an odd number of distinct letters, each representing one of its 45 generators. The word must consist of an odd number of letters because each letter represents fifteen bases and an odd number of letters would collectively yield an odd number of bases, which is one of the requirements of a valid pattern. However, not all odd-letter words are patterns because of the additional requirement that each ray occur an even number of times over all the bases of the pattern. Since only complete 15-gons of rays occur in the words that represent valid patterns, the requiremnt that each ray occur an even number of times over the pattern is equivalent to the requirement that every capital letter occur an even number of times over all the lower case letters of a word representing a valid pattern.

To see how to tell whether a word represents a valid pattern or not, consider the word abegkri'. The word consists of an odd number of letters, so it passes the first test. To see if it passes the second test, we transform it as follows:

      abegkri' → (ABST)(ACRT)(AFPT)(AHMT)(BEPS)(CFOR)(EHMO) → (BCEFHMOPRS)2(AT)4 → (150)2(30)4 – 1054

In the first step, each lower case letter is replaced by its set of four capital letters; in the second, all letters occurring the same number of times over the word are collected together, with the number of occurrences attached as a subscript; and in final step each string of letters within brackets to the left of the dash is replaced by fifteen times the number of letters in the string, while the number to the right of the dash is fifteen times the number of letters in the word, with the subcript 4 attached. The last step gives the formula of the pattern, or the number of occurrences of all the rays in it along with the total number of bases in the pattern. The word represents a valid pattern only if all the subscripts to the left of the dash are even and the number to the right of the dash (which is the number of bases) is odd.

The task of picking out all odd-letter words that represent valid patterns has been made easy for you. Simply enter a 0 or 1 in each of the 29 square boxes below and click the set button to their right. This will make the letters of the pattern appear in the round boxes. Clicking the set button to the right of the round boxes will show how many times each of the capital letters occurs over the word, with even (or odd) counts being shown on a green (or red) background. The validity of the pattern can be confirmed at a glance by seeing that all the square boxes showing letter counts are colored green. Instead of clicking the set button after the round boxes, you could click on the letters one by one and see the pattern build up. You will see red boxes appearing frequently during the buildup, but after all the letters have been clicked only green boxes will appear.

Here is something interesting. After you have called up a pattern, you can "unclick" some of the letters in it, i.e., click on them again to turn them off (making their color change from green to red). You will then be left with a smaller word, or pattern. Very often, this smaller word will not be a valid pattern, as you can see from the red boxes that go with the letter counts below. However, in a few (relatively rare) cases the smaller word will be a valid pattern. When that happens we will say that the original pattern is reducible because it has a smaller pattern embedded in it. If a word represents a valid pattern but has no smaller patterns embedded in it, we will say that it is irreducible. Determining which patterns are irreducible is an interesting problem which will not be addressed here. An example of a reducible pattern is furnished by the word cdnpuvy, which has the formula (210)2 – 1054. Two smaller patterns within it are cduvy and cdnpy, both of which have the formula (150)2 – 754.

In closing, we mention that we have constructed only patterns of a special type, namely, those involving both rays and bases that are multiples of fifteen. The 120-cell houses a large number of other patterns that proving the BKS theorem, but they cannot be displayed using the approach taken here.
